\noindent
Consider two covariance matrices $\AA_{n\times n}$ and $\BB_{n\times n}$.
We say that $\AA$ is \emph{larger} than $\BB$,
denoted by $\AA\succeq \BB$, if $\AA-\BB$ is positive semidefinite.
But why is positive definiteness the right notation to compare the size of covariance matrices?

To understand this,
recall that a covariance matrix is not only symmetric,
but also positive semidefinite.
Let $\xx=(x_1,...,x_n)\T$ be a random vector.
Its covariance matrix is given by:
\begin{align}
	\KK &\coloneqq \E{(\xx-\E{\xx})(\xx-\E{\xx})\T}.
\end{align}
For any fixed vector $\vv\in\reals^n$,
consider the following quadratic form:
\begin{align}
	\vv\T \KK \vv &= \E[\vv\T(\xx-\E{\xx})(\vv\T(\xx-\E{\xx}))\T] \geq 0.
\end{align}
Since this holds for any $\vv$,
the covariance matrix $\KK$ is positive semidefinite by definition.

There is another intuitive way of understanding the positive semidefiniteness.
Consider the same vector $\vv$ and the random vector $\xx$.
The dot product $\zz=\vv\T \xx$ is a (scaled) projection of the random vector from $n$-dimensional
space on a one-dimensional space spanned by $\vv$.
The variance of $\zz$ is given by
\begin{align}
	\var(\zz)
	&= \E[(\vv\T \xx)(\vv\T \xx)\T ] - \E(\vv\T \xx)\E(\vv\T \xx)\T  \\
	&= \vv\T \big(\E(\xx\xx\T ) - \E(\xx)\E(\xx)\T \big)\vv \\
	&= \vv\T \KK\vv.
\end{align}
Thus, the quadratic form $\vv\T\KK\vv$ has a concrete interpretation:
It is the variance of the projection of $\xx$ in the direction $\vv$.
And since variances are always non-negative, the covariance matrix must be positive semidefinite.

With the two interpretations of covariance matrices in mind,
we can now understand how to compare two covariance matrices.
Let $\xx=(x_1,...,x_n)\T$ and $\yy=(y_1,...,y_n)\T$ be random vectors,
both with mean $(0,...,0)\T$ for simplicity.
Let $\AA=\E(\xx\xx\T)$ and $\BB=\E(\yy\yy\T)$ denote their covariance matrices respectively.
The question is how to compare $\AA$ and $\BB$ in a way that reflects the dispersion of the underlying random vectors.

Since it is easy to compare the variances of one-dimensional random variables,
a natural idea is to compare the variances of \emph{all} one-dimensional projections.
Let $\vv\in\reals^n$ be an arbitrary vector
and consider the projection $\vv\T\xx$.
Its variance is given by
\begin{align}
	\E[(\vv\T \xx)^2]
	&= \E(\vv\T \xx\xx\T\vv) \\
	&= \vv\T\E(\xx\xx\T)\vv
	= \vv\T \AA\vv.
\end{align}
Similarly, for $\yy$ we have
\begin{align}
	\E[(\vv\T \yy)^2]
	= \vv\T \BB\vv.
\end{align}
Therefore, if we find that
\begin{align}
	\vv\T \AA\vv - \vv\T \BB\vv
	= \vv\T (\AA - \BB)\vv
	\geq 0
	\quad\forall \vv\in\reals^n,
\end{align}
then matrix $\AA-\BB$ is positive semidefinite by definition.
In other words,
\begin{align}
	\AA \succeq \BB
	\iff
	\vv\T \AA\vv \geq \vv\T \BB\vv
	\quad\forall \vv\in\reals^n.
\end{align}
This defines the \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Loewner_order}{\textit{Loewner order}}
on the set of positive semidefinite matrices.
When $\AA$ and $\BB$ are covariance matrices, the interpretation is especially natural:
\begin{tcolorbox}[enhanced,sharp corners,frame hidden,boxrule=0sp,borderline west={3pt}{0pt}{black!70}]
	If $\AA-\BB$ is positive semidefinite,
	then \emph{in every direction $\vv$},
	the variance of the projection of $\xx$ is at least as large as that of $\yy$.
\end{tcolorbox}

This perspective is also easy to visualize geometrically.
In two dimensions, a covariance matrix can be represented by an ellipse.%
\footnote{%
Representing the covariance matrix of a random vector $\xx\in\reals^n$ by an ellipsoid in $\reals^n$ is natural.
An ellipsoid can be defined as the set of points $\{\vv\in\reals^n: \vv\T \KK \vv \leq 1\}$ for some positive semidefinite matrix $\KK$.
The shape and rotation of the ellipsoid are determined by eigenvalues and eigenvectors of $\KK$,
which in turn correspond to the variances and principal directions of the distribution of $\xx$.
\href{https://arxiv.org/abs/1302.4881}{Friendly, Monette, and Fox (2013)} explores some of these geometric insights in more detail.
}
Suppose the blue ellipse corresponds to $\blue{\xx}$, with covariance matrix $\blue{\AA}$,
and the red ellipse corresponds to $\red{\yy}$, with covariance matrix $\red{\BB}$.

\end{center}
In \textsc{Case 1}, $\blue{\AA}$ is \emph{larger} than $\red{\BB}$
since the blue ellipse completely contains the red ellipse,
indicating that in \emph{every direction}, the variance of $\blue{\xx}$ exceeds that of $\red{\yy}$.
However, the same cannot be said in \textsc{Case 2}.
Along some directions, such as $\vv_1$, the variance of $\blue{\xx}$ is larger;
along other directions, such as $\vv_2$, the variance of $\red{\yy}$ is larger.
In this case, $\blue{\AA}$ and $\red{\BB}$ are not comparable under the Loewner order,
meaning that there is no clear way to say that one covariance matrix is larger than the other.
